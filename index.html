Introduction to Data Communication

What is Data Communication?
Data communication is the process of transferring data between two or more devices through a transmission medium. For communication to occur, there must be a sender, receiver, message, transmission medium, and protocol.

  • Network Models: The OSI (7-layer) and TCP/IP models define how data travels across networks — from physical hardware up to application-level services.
  • Physical Layer & Media: Covers the actual transmission of raw bits over physical media including twisted pair cables, coaxial cable, fiber optics, and wireless channels.
  • Data and Signals: Data exists in analog or digital forms. Signals — the representation of data — can be analog (continuous waveforms) or digital (discrete pulses). Key properties include amplitude, frequency, and phase.
⏳

Evolution of Data Communication

  • Early Systems: Humans have always needed to communicate over distance. Smoke signals, semaphore flags, and light-based heliographs were among the earliest methods, encoding information through visual patterns.
  • Electrical Systems: The telegraph marked a revolution — information could now travel at the speed of electricity through wires, breaking the physical barrier of distance.
  • Modern Digital Networks: The internet, mobile networks, and fibre optic cables represent today's digital infrastructure — enabling near-instantaneous global communication.
  • Core Principles: Throughout all eras, the fundamentals remain: a sender encodes a message, transmits it through a medium (via a signal), and a receiver decodes it. What has changed is speed, distance, and capacity.
  • Legacy Influence: Concepts like encoding, modulation, and signal transmission originated in early systems and are still foundational in modern protocols.
🔐

Pretty Good Privacy (PGP) & Secure Communication

PGP is an encryption programme that provides cryptographic privacy and authentication for digital communications. It is widely used for securing emails and files.

  • Encryption/Decryption: PGP encrypts data using a public key; only the holder of the corresponding private key can decrypt it.
  • Digital Signatures: A sender signs a message with their private key, allowing recipients to verify authenticity and detect tampering.
  • Key Management: PGP uses a "web of trust" — users exchange public keys and sign each other's keys to establish identity.
  • Symmetric + Asymmetric: PGP combines both — it uses asymmetric (RSA) cryptography to exchange a session key, then symmetric (AES) for the actual data, balancing security and speed.
  • Security Goals: Confidentiality (only intended recipient reads it), Integrity (message unchanged), Authentication (verified sender), Non-repudiation (sender cannot deny sending).
  • Limitations: Complex key management, susceptibility if private key is compromised, and usability challenges for non-technical users.
🛰️

Satellite Communication Systems

Satellite communication relays signals between distant points on Earth via orbiting spacecraft, enabling global coverage beyond terrestrial networks.

  • Components: Ground station (uplink transmitter/receiver), satellite transponder (receives, amplifies, and retransmits), and downlink to the destination.
  • Signal Path: Data travels from a transmitting ground station up to the satellite (uplink), the transponder converts and boosts the frequency, then sends it down (downlink) to a receiving station.
  • Orbit Types: GEO (35,786 km) — stationary appearance, ideal for TV broadcasting; MEO — used by GPS; LEO (500–2,000 km) — low latency, used by Starlink.
  • Advantages: Wide geographic coverage, ability to reach remote areas, and excellent broadcast capability.
  • Limitations: High latency in GEO orbits (~600ms round-trip), signal attenuation due to rain or interference, and significant deployment costs.

Telegraph & Morse Code

The electric telegraph was a 19th-century invention that transmitted coded messages over long distances through electrical pulses in a wire. A telegraph key made and broke the circuit to form signals; operators at the receiving end decoded clicks into text.

Morse Code represents letters and numbers as combinations of short (·) and long (–) signals — dots and dashes.

Extending Hub-Based Ethernet to Wireless Using APs & PoE

  • Hub-Based Ethernet: A hub is a shared medium — all devices on a hub share the same bandwidth. It uses CSMA/CD (Carrier Sense Multiple Access / Collision Detection) to manage simultaneous transmissions.
  • Wireless Networks (CSMA/CA): Wireless cannot detect collisions like wired networks, so it uses CSMA/CA (Collision Avoidance) — devices wait for a clear channel before transmitting and use RTS/CTS to reduce collisions.
  • Access Point (AP): The AP bridges the wired (Ethernet) and wireless segments. Devices connect wirelessly to the AP, which forwards their traffic onto the wired network.
  • Power over Ethernet (PoE): PoE delivers electrical power along with data through standard Ethernet cables, eliminating the need for separate power supplies for APs.
  • Deployment Strategies: AP placement should consider coverage overlap, interference, the number of users, and physical obstructions. Proper cabling and VLAN segmentation improve performance.
  • Performance: Moving from a hub to a switch+AP setup reduces collisions and improves throughput significantly. Wireless introduces additional latency and is affected by interference.
